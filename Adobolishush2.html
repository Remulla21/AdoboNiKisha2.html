Fun Facts About Adobo

  • Ancient Filipino Roots: Long before the Spanish arrived in the Philippines, indigenous Filipinos were already preserving meat by cooking it in vinegar and salt. This early preservation method protected food from spoiling in the tropical climate and became the foundation of what we now know as adobo. This proves that the dish is deeply tied to the practical and resourceful lifestyle of early Filipinos.
  • A Spanish Name, But Not a Spanish Dish: Although the term “adobo” was given by Spanish colonizers—because the dish reminded them of their own “adobar” marinade—the cooking process itself is purely Filipino. The technique existed centuries earlier, making adobo an authentic pre-colonial dish with a name added later for convenience.
  • The Unofficial National Dish: While the Philippines has not officially declared a national dish, adobo is widely embraced as the country’s unofficial culinary symbol. Its popularity spans from Luzon to Mindanao, and it is one of the most commonly cooked dishes in Filipino households. Adobo’s simplicity, affordability, and rich flavor have cemented its place as a national favorite.
  • A Dish With Endless Variations: Adobo is incredibly versatile. There are countless versions across the country—adobo sa gata (with coconut milk), adobong dilaw (yellow adobo using turmeric), adobong puti (white adobo using only vinegar and no soy sauce), spicy adobo, sweet adobo, and vegetable variations such as adobong sitaw or adobong kangkong. Every region, and even every household, has its own special twist.
  • A Symbol of Home and Family: For many Filipinos—especially Overseas Filipino Workers—adobo represents comfort, nostalgia, and the warmth of home. The aroma of soy sauce, vinegar, and garlic cooking on the stove often brings back memories of family meals, celebrations, and simple moments shared at the dining table.
  • A Global Filipino Icon: Adobo has gone beyond Filipino kitchens. It appears in international cookbooks, food documentaries, travel vlogs, restaurant menus, and even films. Because of its bold flavors and long history, adobo has become one of the most recognizable and celebrated Filipino dishes around the world.
